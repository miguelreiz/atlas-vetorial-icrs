# Chapter 4 — The Three Domains of ICRS Assessment


> **Key Points**
> - AVBC decomposes ICRS planning into **three sequential modules**: Optical (O), Topographic (T), and Biomechanical (B).
> - **Module O** gates the process: evaluates optical coherence (ACE index) to predict functional improvement.
> - **Module T** locates the ectasia and identifies the **Mechanical Neutral Axis** (MNA).
> - **Module B** maps clinical need to specific biomechanical vectors (VR, VT, Vτ) and ring parameters.
> - The trimodal structure ensures **transparent, traceable, and calibratable** decision-making.

---

## 4.1 Introduction

The clinical assessment of a keratoconic cornea before intracorneal ring segment (ICRS) implantation has traditionally followed a binary paradigm: the surgeon determines whether ectasia is present and, if so, selects a ring configuration from an empirical nomogram. This approach reduces the complexity of corneal biomechanics to a mere table exercise. This chapter presents a trimodal and systematic assessment model — designated by the **Optical (O)**, **Topographic (T)**, and **Biomechanical (B)** domains.

![Figure 4.1 — The AVBC trimodal paradigm: sequential O → T → B assessment.](book_figures/fig_04_01_paradigma_otb.svg)


The three structural questions are:

1. **Will the patient see better? (O Module)** The highest priority is optical. If the patient's visual degradation is not caused by the aberrations that the ring can correct (coma and low-order astigmatism), the surgery will fail functionally.
2. **Where is the ectasia? (T Module)** Topography is not just K-max. The focus should be the **Mechanical Neutral Axis (MNA)** — the true meridian of structural weakness.
3. **How should the surgeon act? (B Module)** The surgical intent is mapped onto the vector triad: Flatten (VR), Regularize (VT), or Reposition (Vτ).

> [!NOTE]
> **For the Clinician: The O-T-B Paradigm**
> Never skip steps. Many surgeons jump directly to ring geometry (B Module) based solely on a tangential map (T Module), forgetting to ask whether the patient's optical aberrations are even "ring-amenable" (O Module). A ring in an eye with dominant trefoil aberration will not improve vision, even if it flattens the cornea.

## 4.2 O Module — Optical Coherence Assessment

### 4.2.1 Rationale and the Axial Coherence Index (ACE)

The theoretical basis for the O Module lies in aberrometry. The intrastromal ring is fundamentally a low- and medium-order tool: it acts massively on defocus (Z₂⁰), astigmatism (Z₂²), and coma (Z₃¹). Very high-order aberrations or irregular astigmatisms caused by epithelial scars remain untouched.

To link topography to vision, the AVBC introduces the **Axial Coherence Index (ACEmin)**, which measures the angle between the steepest axis (topographic) and the coma axis (optical):
$$ACE_{min} = |\theta_{topo} - \theta_{coma}|$$

### 4.2.2 Assessment Criteria

The O Module evaluates optical coherence using four central parameters (6 mm pupil):

**Table 5.1.** Optical screening criteria.
| Criterion | Favorable (O+) | Intermediate (O~) | Unfavorable (O−) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ACEmin (°)** | < 15° (High ACE) | 15°–45° (Moderate) | > 45° (Low ACE) |
| **Coma RMS (μm)** | < 2.50 | 2.50–3.50 | > 3.50 |
| **Δ Axis (Refraction vs K)** | < 15° | 15°–30° | > 30° |
| **Total HOA RMS (μm)** | < 2.0 | 2.0–4.0 | > 4.0 |

> [!WARNING]
> **For the Clinician: Golden Rule of the O Module**
> A patient classified as **O−** (Unfavorable) on *any* criterion is not a good candidate for primary ICRS. No amount of nomogram optimization will save the outcome. The appropriate referral would be DALK, scleral lenses, or a combined surgery (e.g., CAIRS + Cross-linking).

Studies with over 1,100 eyes proved that patients with High ACE (ACEmin < 15°) have a corrected visual acuity gain 2.6 times greater than patients with Low ACE (> 45°).

## 4.3 T Module — Topographic Morphology

### 4.3.1 Morphological Archetypes

![Figure 4.2 — The 4 topographic morphological archetypes + keratoglobus (Module T).](book_figures/fig_04_02_arquetipos_topograficos.svg)


Topographic analysis goes beyond K-max, which is frequently an artifact of conic protrusion. The T Module requires classification into spatial archetypes, which will dictate the macro configuration of the implant:
1. **Central Oval:** Symmetric. Requires concentric flattening (symmetric segments).
2. **Inferior Nipple:** Displaced. Coma dominates and requires asymmetric rings for repositioning (the thicker segment pushes the cone upward).
3. **Paracentral Crescent:** Astigmatism dominance. Requires long rings (angular sweep > 210°) to "belt" the increasing astigmatism.
4. **Globe (> 60 D):** Severe diffuse ectasia. Generally poor response to ICRS.

### 4.3.2 The Mechanical Neutral Axis (MNA)

The biomechanical innovation of this module is the replacement of the Steepest Axis with the **Mechanical Neutral Axis (MNA)** in implant meridian planning.
The MNA is the axis where maximum posterior stromal displacement occurs. It can be clinically estimated by locating the peak on the Posterior Elevation difference map (BFS).

> [!TIP]
> **For the Clinician: Stop Implanting on the Steepest Axis**
> Approximately 40% of ectatic corneas have a divergence greater than 10° between the "reddest" axis on anterior topography (K-steep) and the actual structural weakness (Posterior Elevation). If you implant the ring aligned to K-steep in an eccentric cone, you will lose approximately 15% of regularization capacity. **Always align the incision and implant bisector with the MNA!**

## 4.4 B Module — Biomechanical Selection (Volumetric School)

The B Module translates clinical intent into the three vectors validated by the massive computational campaign (FEBio, N=377), consolidating the empirical-to-**Volumetric School** transition:

**Table 5.2.** The AVBC Biomechanical Triad.
| Clinical Intent | Vector | Biomechanical Driver | Ring Control Parameter |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Flatten** | **VR** | Total Volume Injection | Implant volume (Thickness and Arc) |
| **Regularize** | **VT** | Circumferential Belting | Arc Length |
| **Reposition** | **Vτ** | Torque Moment | Gradient (Left-Right Asymmetry) |

### 4.4.1 VR: The Flattening Vector (Volume)

The fatal error of traditional nomograms was treating the ICRS merely as an elastic belt (the Displacement Paradox). The B Module formally adopts the volumetric law: flattening (VR, clinically measured as ΔK) is driven by **Injected Volume**.
The more solid volume inserted under the mid-peripheral lamellae, the more these are physically pushed forward, resulting in compensatory stretching and flattening of the central cornea.
Therefore, to maximize flattening, the surgeon must maximize Volume: either by increasing *thickness* or by increasing *arc length* (e.g., a 210° ring flattens the spherical equivalent much more than a 160° ring with the same thickness, because it injects more material).

### 4.4.2 VT: The Regularization Vector (Belting)

To reduce irregular astigmatism (cylinder), the ring acts as a restriction barrier (belting). The relaxation stress (VT) falls perfectly linearly with increasing arc length. If the cone is a wide crescent inducing diffuse astigmatism, long arcs (e.g., 320° or 210°+90°) are needed to uniformly intercept the deformed circumferential fibers.

### 4.4.3 Vτ: The Repositioning Vector (Asymmetry)

If topography shows an Inferior Nipple cone (high coma aberration), the optical center is decentered. Symmetric rings never correct this (Vτ = 0). The surgeon must invoke the Torque Vector through asymmetric rings (e.g., progressive 150–300 μm or pairs 200/300). The thickness gradient (Mass Differential) causes a lever force that physically repels the cone away from the thicker side, centralizing vision.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **For the Clinician: Operational Summary of the B Module**
> 1. Need to reduce myopia (high K-mean)? **Inject Volume** (thicker segments or larger arcs).
> 2. Need to eliminate astigmatism? **Belt** (210° or 320° arcs distribute stresses homogeneously).
> 3. Need to centralize the cone (reduce coma)? **Create Asymmetry** (place the thick part of the ring where the cone is, to push it back to center).

### 4.4.4 Pachymetric Modulator
Thinner corneas (< 430 μm) react with biomechanically more violent response (amplification of ~20% in VR) to the same inserted volume than thick corneas (> 500 μm). The same ring configuration in a very thin eye generates overcorrection.

## 4.5 Integrated Clinical Flow (Documentation)

The AVBC protocol finalizes the consultation by condensing the analysis into an auditable surgical annotation:

**Example AVBC Profile:**
> `[O+] | T: Inferior Crescent, MNA 260° | B: High Vol, Wide Belting, No Asymmetry -> Arc 320° Symmetric (200 µm)`

1. The coherence proof was validated (O+).
2. The implant axis targets the posterior structural elevation (MNA 260°), ignoring epithelial surface artifacts.
3. Biomechanics prescribed a single long circular bar (320°) because the B Module demands maximum belting (to treat the crescent pattern's astigmatism) with moderate volume (to avoid over-flattening myopia). Without need for drastic apex repositioning, progressive rings are dispensed with.

## Didactic Summary

- The AVBC framework decomposes ICRS planning into **three sequential modules**: Optical (O), Topographic (T), and Biomechanical (B).
- **Module O** acts as a gatekeeper, evaluating optical coherence to predict visual outcomes.
- **Module T** provides spatial localization and introduces the **MNA** as a key reference axis.
- **Module B** translates clinical needs into biomechanical vector prescriptions.
- This trimodal structure ensures every recommendation is **transparent, traceable, and calibratable**.
