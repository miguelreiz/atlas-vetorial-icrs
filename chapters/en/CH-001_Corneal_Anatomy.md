## 📖 INSTRUCTIONAL CONTENT

### Introduction

Before understanding **how** an intracorneal ring works, you must understand **where** it works. This chapter presents only the anatomy directly relevant to the ring surgeon — without excess, without a full histology review. The focus is surgical.

### The Cornea in 60 Seconds

The human cornea is a transparent lens ~550 μm thick centrally and ~700 μm in the periphery. It contributes approximately **70% of the eye's total refractive power** (~43 D out of ~60 D total).

For the ring surgeon, the cornea can be understood as a structure with **3 functional layers**:

| Layer | Thickness | Relevance to ICRS |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Epithelium + Bowman's** | ~50 μm | Not directly involved. Superficial healing. |
| **Stroma** | ~450 μm (90%) | **TARGET LAYER.** The ring is implanted here, at ~70-80% depth. |
| **Descemet's + Endothelium** | ~15 μm | Critical protection. Should never be reached by the tunnel. |

![Figure 1.1: Corneal Cross Section — The corneal layers highlighting the ring implantation zone at ~70-80% stromal depth](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/cornea_cross_section_en.png)

### The Stroma: The Layer That Matters

The corneal stroma is composed of ~200–250 superimposed collagen lamellae. Each lamella is an extremely thin "sheet" of collagen fibers arranged in precise patterns.

Why does this matter for rings?
Because the intracorneal ring does not act as a rigid mechanical bridge (like a titanium plate on a fractured bone). It acts by **tensioning the collagen fibers**.

Imagine the stroma as a hammock made of loose ropes (the keratoconic cornea). When you insert a rigid triangular or semi-circular block (the ring) between the ropes, you force the ropes passing above and below it to take a longer path. The result? **They stretch.**

This tension generated at the ring propagates through the lamellae and is transmitted back to the anterior surface of the cornea, "pulling" it and altering its macroscopic curvature.

![Figure 1.2: Microscopic View of the Stroma — The PMMA block acts as a mechanical separator, tensioning the adjacent collagen fibers. This tension restructures the surface curvature (block effect).](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/collagen_tension_mechanism_en.png)

**Crucial fact for ICRS:** Not all lamellae are equal.

- **Anterior (superficial) lamellae:** Interlaced, dense, strongly connected to each other. Difficult to separate. Respond less to the ring (high rigidity).
- **Posterior (deep) lamellae:** More parallel, less interlaced, with more ground substance between them. **Easier to separate.** This is where the ring tunnel is created.

> **Anatomical Pearl — Why 70-75% Depth?**
>
> The ring is implanted in the posterior stroma (~70-75%) for **architectural and biomechanical** reasons, not just technical ones:
>
> **Lamellar Architecture:** The anterior stroma (1/3) has interlaced and oblique fibers — a dense felt-like network, difficult to dissect. The posterior stroma (2/3) has **parallel** lamellae, predominantly along the nasal-temporal and superior-inferior axes (WAXS: 66% within ±22.5°). Lamellae separate along natural planes → the tunnel is easily created in this zone. The ease is a **consequence** of the architecture, not the primary reason.
>
> **Tenting–Anchor Equilibrium:** The primary reason is biomechanical. The ring acts as a wedge/fulcrum: it pushes the tissue *above* anteriorly (tenting = RV), while the tissue *below* provides the reactive anchoring. The optimal point balances both:
>
> | Depth | Above the ring | Below (anchor) | Effect |
> |-------|----------------|----------------|--------|
> | 60% | 60% | 40% | Weak RV — too little tissue above for tenting |
> | **70-75%** ✅ | 70-75% | 25-30% | **Optimal** — maximum tenting + sufficient anchor |
> | >80% | >80% | <20% | **Decreased** RV (FEM) — posterior anchor too thin |
>
> Above 80%, the ring "floats" without adequate posterior resistance — the flattening effect drops. The phrase "deeper is always better" is a dangerous myth.

### Corneal Geometry: The Map the Surgeon Reads

The cornea can be mapped into concentric zones. **Each zone has a different fibrillar architecture** — and this determines how each ring diameter interacts with the tissue:

#### Collagen Fibril Dimensions and the WAXS Technique

> **Understanding the Tool:** **WAXS** = *Wide-Angle X-ray Scattering*. It is the cutting-edge technique used by biophysicists like Keith Meek and Craig Boote to map the orientation of collagen fibers in the cornea at the nanometric scale. It is the WAXS maps that reveal where the fibers are orthogonal (center) and where they turn tangential (periphery). This technology is the fundamental scientific basis for the geometric correspondence demonstrated in this Atlas.

| Parameter | Value | Reference |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Fibril diameter** | 30-35 nm | ✅ Meek & Boote, 2004 |
| **Fibril length** | ~25 µm | ✅ Meek, 2009 |
| **Interfibrillar spacing** | 40-60 nm (controlled by proteoglycans) | ✅ Meek & Knupp, 2015 |
| **Thickness of each lamella** | ~2 µm | ✅ Radner et al., 1998 |
| **Width of each lamella** | 0.5–2.5 mm (variable) | ✅ Winkler et al., 2013 |
| **Total number of lamellae** | ~200 (stacked) | ✅ Komai & Ushiki, 1991 |

![Figure 1.12a: Collagen Fibril Dimensions — The lamellar microstructure revealed by WAXS.](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/Figura_Fibrilas_WAXS_en.png)

#### Fibrillar Architecture by Zone — The Key to Understanding Ring Diameters

| Zone | Diameter | Dominant Fibers | Orientation | Relevance for ICRS |
|------|----------|-----------------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Central** | 0–3 mm | 🔴 Orthogonal radials | N-T (0°) and S-I (90°), **66% within ±22.5°** of the axes | Optical zone — DO NOT implant here. This is the target for flattening (RV). Fibrils maintain constant diameter. |
| **Paracentral** | 3–5 mm | 🔴→🔵 Transition | Orthogonal → isotropic (fibers spread out) | **Ø5mm Ring** — intercepts mixed zone. Strong RV, moderate TV. Fibrils begin to alter. |

| **Limbus** | 9–12 mm | 🔵🔵🔵 Limbal Annulus | Pure circumferential (posterior 30% of stroma) | Do not implant here. Anatomical reference for incision |

> **⚠️ Depth Qualification on WAXS (important for scientific defense):**
>
> The WAXS maps from Meek & Boote (2004) represent the **sum of the entire corneal thickness** — the X-ray passes through the entire cornea. However, the recorded N-T/S-I orthogonal pattern is **dominated by the contribution of the posterior stroma** (70% of thickness, more organized fibers). Winkler et al. (2013) confirmed via SHG that the posterior stroma is much more strongly orthogonal than the anterior. **Conclusion:** the zonal maps (orthogonal center → circumferential limbus) are directly representative of the ICRS interaction zone at 70-75%, since the preferential orientation comes primarily from the posterior stroma where the ring is implanted.

### The Stromal Proportion Explains PTA — The Fibrillar Bridge Hypothesis

> **💡 AUTHOR'S SYNTHESIS — Original Hypothesis**
>
> The following section proposes an unprecedented bridge between two independent bodies of evidence: fibrillar anatomy (Meek/Winkler) and Santhiago's PTA index. Each component is a published fact; **the causal connection is proposed by the author of this Atlas.**

#### What is PTA (Percent Tissue Altered)

PTA was proposed by Marcony R. Santhiago as a predictor of post-LASIK ectasia (✅ Santhiago MR et al., *Ophthalmology*, 2014):

```
PTA = (Flap Thickness + Ablation Depth) / Central Corneal Thickness (CCT)
```

| PTA | Interpretation | Evidence |
|-----|----------------|----------|
| <35% | Safe | ✅ Santhiago 2014 |
| 35-40% | Gray zone | ✅ Santhiago 2014 |
| **≥40%** | **Independent risk of ectasia** | ✅ Santhiago 2014, Ophthalmology |

**Clinical findings (✅ published facts):**
- PTA ≥40% is the **most robust and independent** predictor of post-LASIK ectasia (✅ Santhiago MR et al., *Ophthalmology*, 2014)
- Superior to RSB (residual stromal bed) alone as a predictor (✅ Santhiago MR et al., *JCRS*, 2014)
- Superior to Randleman's ERSS (Ectasia Risk Scoring System) (✅ Santhiago MR et al., 2014)
- The anterior **40%** of the cornea has **significantly higher** tensile strength than the posterior 60% (✅ Randleman JB et al., *JCRS*, 2008)

#### The Numerical Coincidence — Why 40%?

The fundamental question: **why exactly 40%?** Marcony Santhiago answered this in his primary studies pointing out that tensile strength is not uniform: the anterior third is biomechanically much stronger than the posterior two-thirds (based on Randleman's 2008 studies).

However, Santhiago described the "what" (tensile strength), but the microstructural "why" was missing. Tensile strength does not appear out of nowhere; it is the physical product of an invisible architecture. The exact structural answer lies in the **proportion and location of interlamellar oblique fibers** mapped by Winkler et al.:

| Anatomical Data | Value | Clinical Data | Value |
|-----------------|-------|---------------|-------|
| ✅ Anterior stroma (dense 🟢 obliques) | **~30-40%** of thickness | ✅ PTA ectasia threshold | **≥40%** |
| ✅ Anterior tensile strength >> posterior | Anterior 40% concentrates highest rigidity | ✅ RSB alone does not predict ectasia | RSB is pure posterior |
| ✅ Obliques prevent interlamellar shear | Radner 1998, Winkler 2013 | ✅ Post-LASIK ectasia = same phenotype as KC | Same morphology and progression |

> **💡 Author's Hypothesis:** The PTA ≥40% threshold marks the point where the ENTIRE layer rich in oblique fibers (anterior stroma) has been **cut** (by the flap) and/or **removed** (by the ablation). Below this point, the RSB is pure posterior stroma — parallel lamellae without interlamellar locking — vulnerable to sliding under IOP.

#### Pathogenesis Cascade: PTA in Fiber Language

```
✅ LASIK FLAP (~120-160 µm) cuts THROUGH the anterior stroma
   ↓
✅ Flap BREAKS the 🟢 interlamellar oblique fibers (Winkler 2013)
   → Destroys the "felt" network that prevents shear between lamellae
   ↓
✅ Laser ABLATION (~50-100 µm) REMOVES tissue at the interface
   → Eliminates additional oblique and radial fibers
   ↓
🔬 The residual bed (RSB) = POSTERIOR stroma
   → Contains only 🔴 parallel fibers (N-T/S-I orthogonal radials)
   → NO 🟢 obliques → NO resistance to interlamellar shear
   ↓
💡 If PTA ≥40%:
   → The ENTIRE oblique-rich layer was compromised
   → The RSB is composed of posterior lamellae WITH NO transverse locking
   → IOP acts on "loose" lamellae (same situation as keratoconus)
   → Lamellae slide → ECTASIA
```

When PTA exceeds ~40%, the locking network ("felt") is entirely bypassed, leaving posterior lamellae loose.](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/Figura_Cascata_PTA_Fibras_en.png)

#### 💡 DISCUSSION BOX: Is PTA the Same in PRK and LASIK? — A Fibrillar Provocation

> **⚠️ IMPORTANT NOTE:** Santhiago's original studies (2014) defined and validated PTA exclusively for **LASIK**. PTA was not formally studied in PRK in the original articles. The following discussion is a **hypothetical extension proposed by the author**, applying fibrillar logic to the mechanical difference between the two procedures.

##### The Formula is the Same — The Effect is Not

```
PTA_LASIK = (Flap Thickness + Ablation) / CCT
PTA_PRK   = (0 + Ablation) / CCT        ← NO FLAP
```

Numerically, a PTA of 30% in PRK looks "less" than 40% in LASIK. But the fibrillar question runs deeper: **what does each procedure do to the obliques 🟢?**

##### The Double Insult of LASIK vs. The Single Insult of PRK

| Mechanism | LASIK | PRK |
|-----------|-------|-----|
| **Horizontal cutting of obliques** | ✅ The flap (~120-160 µm) creates a **horizontal shear plane** that sections ALL obliques at that depth | ❌ **NO horizontal cut** |
| **Removal of obliques** | ✅ Ablation removes tissue from the bed (post-flap) | ✅ Ablation removes superficial tissue (includes obliques) |
| **Type of damage** | **Section + Removal** (double insult) | **Removal only** (single insult) |
| **Remaining obliques** | Obliques **above the flap** are disconnected from the stroma below — the flap NEVER regenerates obliques (✅ Dawson et al., 2008) | Remaining obliques maintain **intact vertical continuity** — no shear plane |
| **"Felt" network integrity** | ❌ **Split** — the felt is cut into two "mats" that never reconnect | ⚠️ **Thinned** — the felt becomes thinner, but the remaining vertical connections remain functional |

##### 💡 Author's Hypothesis: The Effective PTA is Different

From the fibrillar perspective, the same PTA number creates different damage:

```
PTA 35% in LASIK (flap 120 + abl 72 / CCT 550):
  → Flap sections ALL obliques down to 120 µm
  → Ablation removes an additional 72 µm
  → Total: 192 µm of damage → HORIZONTAL CUT created
  → "Felt" SPLIT into two disconnected planes

PTA 35% in PRK (ablation 193 / CCT 550):
  → Ablation removes 193 µm from the surface
  → Removes obliques progressively (top to bottom)
  → NO horizontal shear plane
  → "Felt" THINNED but INTACT — residual obliques maintain connection
```

> **💡 Author's Conclusion:** A PTA of 35% in LASIK is biomechanically **more destructive** than a PTA of 35% in PRK, because the flap creates an interlamellar shear plane that the surface ablation of PRK never creates. **The numerical PTA is the same, but the fibrillar damage is qualitatively different.**

##### Indirect Evidence Supporting This Hypothesis

1. **Post-PRK ectasia is vastly rarer** than post-LASIK, even with deep ablations (✅ widely acknowledged clinical fact)
2. **PRK epithelial healing preserves** the continuity of Bowman's/anterior stroma in a way that LASIK does not (✅ Netto MV et al., 2006)
3. **The LASIK flap never recovers** the original tensile strength — cut obliques do not regenerate (✅ Dawson DG et al., 2008; Schmack I et al., 2005)

### The 5 Anatomical Landmarks of the Ring Surgeon

1. **Corneal Apex** — The point of maximum curvature. In keratoconus, it almost never coincides with the pupillary center. This is the point the vectors try to reposition.

2. **Pupillary Center** — The functional visual axis. The "target" to which the Coma Vector (TV) should push the apex.

3. **Steepest Axis (K-steep)** — The meridian of greatest topographic curvature. Determines where the incision should be made (Axis Rule).

4. **Tunnel Depth** — 70–80% of pachymetry at the implantation site. Too superficial = risk of extrusion. Too deep = risk of perforation.

5. **Minimum Pachymetry** — The thinnest point of the cornea (usually at the cone's apex). Sets the safety limit for the ring (generally >400 μm minimum at the tunnel site).

### What Changes in Keratoconus?

In keratoconus, the corneal anatomy alters in specific ways that directly affect ring planning:

| Alteration | Clinical Impact |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Focal stromal thinning** | Less safety margin for tunnel depth |
| **Apex displacement** | Generates coma (CV). Requires torque (Vτ) to correct |
| **Disorganized lamellae** | Scarring and stromal opacity can limit post-op transparency |
| **Focally increased curvature** | Elevated K-max. Primary target of the RV |
| **Thickness asymmetry** | Unequal pachymetry across meridians requires asymmetric planning |

### The Collagen Mesh at the Micro Scale — The Invisible Protagonist

> *This is the most important section of this chapter and perhaps the entire Atlas. Everything you will learn in the next 200 pages — vectors, Placido, nomograms — is born here, in the collagen fibers.*

![Figure 1.3: The Multi-Scale Framework — The same cone seen at 3 magnification levels: Placido (macro), Cross Section (meso), Collagen Fibers (micro)](../../images/P1-01_Framework/en/Figura_Framework_MultiEscala_en.png)

#### Normal Lamellar Architecture

The stroma is composed of ~200 stacked lamellae. Each lamella is a "sheet" ~2 µm thick with type I collagen fibrils arranged parallel within each sheet, but crossing at regular angles between adjacent sheets.

**Crossing Pattern (3-Fiber Model — based on WAXS and SHG):**

- 🔴 **Radial fibers:** run from the center to the periphery (like spokes of a wheel) — ✅ Meek & Boote, 2004; Winkler et al., 2013
- 🔵 **Tangential (circumferential) fibers:** run in parallel arcs to the limbus (like concentric fences) — ✅ Meek & Newton, 1998 (Limbal Annulus)
- 🟢 **Oblique (interlamellar) fibers:** connect anterior layers to posterior ones (like tie-rods of a bridge) — ✅ Radner et al., 1998; Winkler et al., 2013

![Figure 1.8: Normal Corneal Collagen Mesh — Top View. Radial fibers (red) from center to periphery, tangential (blue) in concentric arcs at the limbus, oblique (green) connecting layers. Balanced tension = uniform curvature = concentric Placido](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/collagen_fiber_pattern_en.png)

> **💡 Author's Synthesis:** Each fiber family connects to an ICRS correction vector: radials → RV (flattening), tangentials → TV (redistribution), obliques → interlamellar stabilization. This fiber→vector bridge is proposed by the author based on the convergence of WAXS (Meek), SHG (Winkler), and EM (Radner) evidence.

#### Oblique Fiber Gradient — Why the Ring Goes to 70-75%

Oblique fibers are not uniformly distributed. They form a **gradient**: abundant in the anterior stroma ("felt" type), progressively rare in the posterior (parallel lamellae):

![Figure 1.9: Oblique Fiber Gradient in Cross Section — Anterior stroma (1/3) dense in green obliques; posterior stroma (2/3) with parallel lamellae and few obliques. The ring tunnel is created in the oblique-free zone at 70-75% — Based on Winkler et al. (2013) 3D SHG mapping](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/oblique_fiber_gradient_en.png)

#### What Changes in Keratoconus — The Pathogenesis Cascade in the Fibers

In keratoconus, enzymatic degradation (MMP-2, MMP-9 — ✅ Kenney et al., 2004) weakens the proteoglycans anchoring the oblique fibers. The cascade progresses in 4 stages:

![Figure 1.10: Pathogenesis Cascade of Keratoconus at the Fiber Scale — 4 stages: (1) Normal: intact fibers. (2) Degradation: MMPs degrade proteoglycans, obliques lose anchoring. (3) Sliding: lamellae slide, loose radials. (4) Ectasia: IOP focal yielding, protrusion. Limbal tangentials preserved in all stages — Kenney 2003/2004, Radner 1998, Winkler 2013, Meek & Knupp 2015](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/cascata_patogenica_fibras_en.png)

![Figure 1.11: Keratoconus at the Fiber Scale — Top view showing: loose and fragmented inferior radials (🔴), absent obliques (🟢) at the cone zone, preserved limbal tangentials (🔵). Ectasia zone in orange. Explains why keratoconus is central/paracentral, never limbal](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/keratoconus_fiber_damage_en.png)

**In the language of the 3 fibers:**
1. 🟢 **Obliques degrade first** — lost proteoglycans → lamellae lose "rivets" (🔬 indirect evidence: Radner 1998 + Winkler 2013)
2. 🔴 **Radials loosen next** — without oblique locking, IOP focally stretches radials (✅ Meek & Knupp 2015)
3. 🔵 **Limbal tangentials remain intact** — the limbal ring is an independent retaining structure (✅ Meek & Newton 1998)
4. Result: asymmetric tension → focal curvature → Placido rings compressed at the cone zone

> **Note (Skill 0):** The total collagen mass in keratoconus decreases by only ~5%. What happens is not fiber destruction, but **redistribution via sliding** — fibrils slip between and within lamellae, shifting position and orientation without being destroyed.

![Figure 1.4: The Lamellar Catastrophe — From intact fibers to keratoconus in 4 steps: normal → enzymatic degradation → sliding → protrusion](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/Figura_Catastrofe_Lamelar_en.png)

#### Placido As a Mirror of the Mesh

Each reflected Placido ring is, literally, the **reflection of the surface tension generated by the fibers below**.

- **Tense and organized fibers** → smooth surface → regular reflection → concentric rings
- **Loose and disorganized fibers** → irregular surface → distorted reflection → compressed rings

> **The phrase that defines this Atlas:** Placido does not measure curvature. It measures the **state of tension** of the collagen mesh. Every distortion you see in the rings is a fiber that has yielded.

#### Astigmatism: The Mesh That Pulls Unevenly

Corneal astigmatism, at its core, is not an "oval shape" of the surface. It is a **tension difference between two orthogonal meridians** in the collagen mesh.

In the steepest meridian (K-steep), the fibers are under high tension — dense, tight, resistant. In the flattest meridian (K-flat), the fibers are more relaxed — spaced out, with lower elastic preload. This tension asymmetry creates curvature asymmetry: where the mesh is tighter, the surface is steeper; where it is looser, the surface is flatter.

![Figure 1.5: Astigmatism in Fiber Language — Top view of the collagen mesh. Dense, tight fibers on the K-steep meridian (90°) contrast with loose, spaced fibers on the K-flat (180°). Uneven tension = astigmatism.](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/astigmatism_fiber_mesh_en.png)

On Placido, this appears as elliptical rings — compressed along the K-steep and spaced out along the K-flat. Each oval ring is the reflection of a mesh that isn't pulling evenly in all directions.

This perspective is critical to understanding the Tangential Vector (Chapter 5): when the ring is implanted on K-steep, it decongests the hyper-tense fibers of that meridian and redistributes tension to the K-flat — the Coupling Effect. **The ring doesn't "correct" astigmatism directly. It rebalances the tension between meridians.**

> **Pearl:** Astigmatism is a disease of the mesh, not the surface. The surface is the consequence. When the surgeon understands this, they never think about "flattening the K-steep" again — they think about "redistributing tension to the K-flat."

---

#### Why Anterior and Posterior Do Not Agree

If the cornea were a homogeneous shell — same rigidity throughout its thickness — anterior and posterior astigmatism would always have the same axis and magnitude. But the cornea **is not homogeneous**. It has at least two mechanical "floors" with distinct properties.

The **anterior stroma** (~120 µm) features densely interlaced lamellae, with fibers crossing in multiple directions. This architecture confers high tangential rigidity — it resists deformation and redistributes load laterally, like a braided fabric.

The **posterior stroma** has more parallelly organized lamellae, with less interlacing between adjacent layers. It is structurally more deformable — yields more easily under the same load.

![Figure 1.6: Why Anterior and Posterior Do Not Agree — Cross section with true proportions (~120µm anterior vs ~330µm posterior). The same IOP acting on two networks with different stiffness can generate astigmatism axes rotated 15-40° between surfaces.](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/anterior_posterior_v2_en.png)

When IOP — which is the same for both floors — deforms the cornea, **each layer responds according to its architecture**. The more flexible posterior stroma might yield on a slightly different axis than the anterior one. The stiffer anterior stroma redistributes part of the load and might maintain an apparent axis different from the true one.

The result is measurable on the Pentacam: angular divergence of 15–40° between anterior and posterior astigmatism axes. This is not measurement error. It is the signature of two distinct mechanical networks responding to the same force differently.

> **Surgical Implication (explored in Chapter 8, Limitation 4):** When anterior and posterior diverge significantly, Placido — which reads only the anterior surface — might not reveal the true vectorial axis of the ectasia. Posterior tomography and the coma axis become mandatory supplements.

---

#### Ectasia as a Layered Failure

Corneal ectasia — whether primary keratoconus or post-refractive surgery ectasia — is not an instantaneous event. It is a progressive collapse that manifests differently in each layer of the mesh.

**Stage 1 — The posterior shifts anteriorly.** Proteoglycan degradation weakens the posterior lamellae first (less interlaced, less resistant). The posterior mesh begins to yield focally — but, contrary to what intuition suggests, the posterior surface doesn't bulge outward. It **bulges toward the anterior surface**, focally reducing stromal thickness. This is the reason why posterior elevation and minimum pachymetry are the first markers to alter on the Pentacam, before the anterior surface shows significant abnormalities.

**Stage 2 — The anterior compensates.** The anterior lamellae, with their dense interlacing, laterally redistribute the tension. They "hold" the surface for longer, partially masking the deformation already existing in depth. Placido can look almost normal while the posterior is already significantly altered.

**Stage 3 — The epithelium masks.** The corneal epithelium acts as a biological smoothing layer — it thickens over depressions and thins over protrusions. This epithelial remodeling (visible on OCT epithelial thickness maps) creates an anterior surface more regular than the underlying stroma actually is. Placido, reflecting this smoothed surface, can underestimate the true severity of the ectasia.

![Figure 1.7: Ectasia as Layered Failure — Progression in 4 stages: intact mesh → posterior shifts anteriorly → anterior redistributes → epithelium masks. In ectasia, the posterior surface bulges toward the anterior (does not protrude outward). Placido only reads the smoothed final surface.](../../images/CH-001_Anatomia/en/ectasia_progression_v2_en.png)

> **Fundamental Lesson for the Ring Surgeon:** What you see on Placido is the final result of a chain of compensations. The anterior surface is the last domino to fall — and when it falls, it's already visible. But the biomechanical vector that caused the ectasia often started on the posterior plane, on an axis that may differ from what the surface shows. This is why advanced vectorial planning (Chapter 8) integrates Placido, posterior tomography, and coma — never one alone.

### Anatomical Pitfalls

1. **Center pachymetry is NOT the implantation site pachymetry.** The ring is implanted in the periphery, where the cornea is thicker. Always measure pachymetry at the intended tunnel site, not just centrally.

2. **The topographic apex is NOT the pupillary center.** In keratoconus, these two points are frequently separated by 1–3 mm. This distance is the Coma Vector (CV).

3. **Anterior stroma ≠ posterior stroma.** Biomechanical properties drop drastically with depth. An implanted ring at 60% versus 80% depth will have completely different mechanical effects.
